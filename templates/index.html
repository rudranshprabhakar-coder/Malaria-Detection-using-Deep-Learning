What is Malaria?

Malaria is a life-threatening disease spread to humans by some types of mosquitoes. It is mostly found in tropical countries and is preventable and curable.

The infection is caused by a parasite and does not spread from person to person. Symptoms can be mild or life-threatening, including fever, chills, headache, fatigue, confusion, seizures, and difficulty breathing.

Did you know? Early diagnosis and treatment of malaria reduces disease, prevents deaths, and contributes to reducing transmission.

Key Facts (2024)

282 Million Cases

Global malaria cases estimated in 2024, a 3% increase from 2023

610,000 Deaths

Estimated malaria deaths in 2024, up from 598,000 in 2023

75% Children Under 5

Account for about 75% of all malaria deaths in the WHO African Region

95% in Africa

WHO African Region carries 95% of global malaria cases

Primary Vector

Spread mainly through bites of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes

10-15 Days

Symptoms usually appear within 10-15 days after mosquito bite

Symptoms & Risk

Common Symptoms

  • Fever
  • Headache
  • Chills
  • Extreme Fatigue
  • Impaired Consciousness
  • Difficulty Breathing

High-Risk Groups

  • Infants & Children Under 5
  • Pregnant Women
  • Travellers
  • People with HIV/AIDS
  • Elderly Population
  • Immunocompromised Persons

Prevention Methods

Mosquito Nets

Use insecticide-treated mosquito nets when sleeping in malaria-risk areas

Insect Repellents

Use DEET, IR3535, or Icaridin-based repellents after dusk

Protective Clothing

Wear long sleeves and pants, especially during evening hours

Chemoprophylaxis

Take preventive medications before traveling to endemic areas

Treatment Guidelines

Artemisinin-Based Therapy

Most effective treatment for P. falciparum malaria. Should be administered promptly upon diagnosis.

Chloroquine

Recommended for P. vivax treatment in areas where the parasite remains sensitive.

Supportive Care

Fluid management, fever control, and monitoring for severe complications.

Emergency Response

Intensive care for severe malaria cases with organ complications.

⚠️ Important: Early diagnosis and immediate treatment are crucial. WHO recommends confirming suspected malaria cases through parasite-based diagnostic testing.

Vaccination Programs

WHO-Recommended Vaccines

RTS,S/AS01 (Mosquirix)

Recommended since October 2021. Significantly reduces malaria in young African children.

R21/Matrix-M

Recommended since October 2023. Safe and effective malaria vaccine for routine immunization.
